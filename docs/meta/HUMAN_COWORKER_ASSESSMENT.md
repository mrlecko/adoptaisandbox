## 2) What You Do Exceptionally Well

## A) You drive toward reality, not narrative
You repeatedly asked:
- “does it actually work?”
- “what fails?”
- “what’s missing?”
- “prove it with tests/logs.”

That behavior is rare and valuable. Most teams over-index on roadmap optics; you push for operational truth.

## B) You escalate quality when needed
You did not accept “mostly fine.”  
You demanded:
- deterministic startup,
- regression tests,
- make targets that are “idiot proof,”
- docs that are current and actionable.

This is exactly how fragile prototypes become reliable systems.

## C) You think in systems, not isolated tickets
You naturally connected:
- architecture decisions,
- developer workflow,
- CI behavior,
- deployment friction,
- handover quality,
- interview/evaluator optics.

That cross-layer thinking is senior-level and materially reduces downstream failure.

## D) You ask high-leverage questions
Your strongest prompts were meta-prompts:
- “what are we not reflecting on?”
- “how should requirements have been specified to avoid loops?”
- “how do we transfer agentic advantage?”

These questions improve the process itself, not just this sprint.

## E) You preserve momentum under uncertainty
Even when things were messy (env delays, CI failures, runner/provider complexity), you kept moving the work forward with clear priorities.

## 5) Blind Spots / Friction Risks (No-BS)

These are growth edges, not deal-breakers:

## A) Pace can outstrip sequencing discipline
You can move so fast that optional complexity (new providers, telemetry, deployment breadth) enters before baseline hardening is fully complete.

Risk:
- more loops than necessary,
- harder root-cause isolation.

Mitigation:
- enforce explicit phase gates (“baseline green before breadth”).

## B) Context switching can multiply moving parts
You can run architecture, infra, UX, tests, and docs in parallel aggressively.

Risk:
- drift across status/docs/contracts unless tightly synchronized.

Mitigation:
- single source-of-truth status map + strict change checklist per cycle.

## C) High standards can pressure collaborators
Your bar is rightly high, but less mature teammates/agents may interpret directness as churn pressure.

Mitigation:
- pair hard standards with explicit “minimum acceptable increment” definitions.

## 8) Suggested Growth Plan (High ROI)

If you want to level up further, focus on these:

1. **Sequencing as a first-class discipline**
   - codify gates early (baseline, tests, docs, CI, then expansion).

2. **Evidence-first planning**
   - define required proof artifacts before implementation starts.

3. **Decision compression**
   - for each major branch, force a quick decision memo:
     - options,
     - tradeoff,
     - chosen path,
     - “revisit when” condition.

4. **Explicit collaborator contracts**
   - especially with agents and cross-functional teammates:
     - required inputs,
     - expected output shape,
     - done definition.
